Case Study — User Research & Product Design

Zero users. One product.
Two very different students to design for.

A product built from zero — no users, no blueprint — that grew to one million students. How Gajino moved from a feature-first content directory to a personal study workspace, built on direct research with the students who actually use it, for ages seven through eighteen.

01 — The Challenge

A directory of files, not a study space.

Gaj is Iran's leading educational publisher. Decades of physical exam books and classroom-based tests formed the backbone of their brand — and the challenge was to bring all of it into a single, scalable digital product. That meant absorbing an existing library of physical content, a set of smaller standalone digital tools, and two very different user types into one coherent experience. No existing users, no inherited patterns to lean on. Gajino was built from the ground up — and the original product suffered from a feature-first rather than user-first architecture. Students across twelve grades were forced into a single, complex navigation structure. The app felt like a file directory, not a personalized place to learn, and young users found it intimidating to navigate.

A playful learning environment designed for students preparing for school exams and national tests — not a dry LMS, but a desk students actually want to sit down at.

02 — How Students Actually Studied

Books, classes, tests, and notes — scattered across separate tools.

Gaj's physical exam books had structured how Iranian students studied for years. The task wasn't just to digitise a content library — it was to take that familiar structure, merge it with existing smaller digital tools, and make it feel natural to open on a phone rather than a desk. Before any redesign began, the team mapped how students actually studied. The components of learning existed, but in the digital space they were disconnected from one another, each living in its own silo.

03 — User Research with Students

Testing moved from office rooms to bookstores and study halls.

Early prototypes were tested directly with students to observe their real behavior and learning habits — not in a sterile lab, but in the places they actually studied, where natural behavior could surface.

04 — The Age Gap

Two students, two completely different ways of discovering content.

The research surfaced a key discovery: primary-school students discover content visually — they don't read labels, they recognize icons and colors, and need constant feedback loops to stay engaged. High-school students discover content functionally — under real academic pressure, they need a deep-work environment with minimal distraction.

Ages 7–11
Discovery is Visual
Fast, icon-driven navigation over text labels
Short attention span — needs immediate, visible reward
Bright, high-contrast visuals strongly preferred
Constant "fun" feedback loops keep engagement alive
Ages 12–18
Discovery is Functional
More structured, content-focused navigation
Low tolerance for childish visuals or feature noise
Under real pressure — wants minimal distraction
Usage patterns closer to adult productivity tools

"Success comes from creating an interface that feels like a toy for a 7-year-old and a professional tool for a 17-year-old — within the same brand."

05 — The "Desk" Epiphany

Students don't organize their study life in folders. They have a desk.

Through behavioral studies, the team realized students don't think in folders or categories — they think in terms of an active desk, where the books and notes they're currently using sit within reach. That desk became the entire interface's central design metaphor.

06 — The Solution

From content library to personal workspace.

The entire UX pivoted from a content library to a personal workspace. Onboarding now starts with an avatar choice that sets the app's tone for that age group. The Digital Desk became a customizable home screen where students pin their active courses — cutting the steps to start a lesson by 40%. Every book on the desk integrates its textbook, tutoring videos, and self-assessment quizzes into one continuous flow, with bite-sized, progress-driven quizzes that turn into instant infographics.

Conclusion

A toy for a 7-year-old. A tool for a 17-year-old. One brand.

Designing for education means balancing two masters at once: the student, who is the user, and the parent, who is the customer. Gajino started with no users, no inherited product, and no template for holding two radically different personas inside one brand. The fact that it grew to one million students isn't just a business number — it's evidence that those two needs aren't in conflict. They just require an interface honest enough to change shape depending on who's sitting down at the desk.
